## Voice Attributes

### 1. Builder, Not Broadcaster

**What it means:** We're in the work, not above it. We share from inside the processâ€”the mess, the half-finished things, the failures. We don't wait until something is polished to talk about it.

**How it affects writing:** Use concrete details. Name the actual thing you built, the bug you hit, the workaround you found. Show your reasoning, not just your conclusions. "I tried X. It broke. So I did Y instead."

**Examples:**
- âœ— "Our plugin leverages AI to optimize your Google Ads workflow."
- âœ“ "I got tired of clicking through the same tabs every morning. So I built a thing that lets Claude do it instead."

- âœ— "We've developed a comprehensive solution for media buyers."
- âœ“ "It handles sitelinks, callouts, and structured snippets. That's it for now. More coming."

**Anti-pattern:** Don't narrate the struggle for sympathy or content. The work speaks. If something was hard, say it once and move on.

### 2. Plain, Not Clever

**What it means:** We explain things simply because that's how we actually think about them. No jargon unless it's genuinely useful. No cleverness for its own sake. Rick Rubin energyâ€”minimal, spacious, trusting the reader to follow.

**How it affects writing:** Short sentences when they work. No padding. If a paragraph can be a sentence, make it a sentence. If a sentence can be cut, cut it. Don't explain what the reader can infer.

**Examples:**
- âœ— "What if I told you there was a paradigm shift happening in the intersection of AI and paid media?"
- âœ“ "I run Google Ads for 25 accounts. Now I do most of it from the terminal."

- âœ— "This isn't just another toolâ€”it's a complete reimagining of how media buyers interact with their campaigns."
- âœ“ "It's a Claude Code plugin for Google Ads. You prompt, it does the work."

**Anti-pattern:** Don't mistake plainness for being terse or cold. Plain can still be warm. It's about removing the performance, not the personality.

### 3. Humble Authority

**What it means:** No one knows anythingâ€”some people just know less than others. We're not the guru on the mountain. We're someone slightly further down the path, leaving markers for whoever wants them. The posture is generous, not superior.

**How it affects writing:** Avoid definitive claims about "the right way." Share what worked for you, acknowledge what you don't know. Give permissionâ€”"you don't need a complex system," "just pick one and start."

**Examples:**
- âœ— "The secret to scaling Google Ads campaigns is..."
- âœ“ "Here's what's been working for me. Your mileage may vary."

- âœ— "Most media buyers are doing it wrong."
- âœ“ "I used to do it this way. Then I tried something different."

**Anti-pattern:** Humble doesn't mean self-deprecating or wishy-washy. You can say "this works" with confidence. Just don't say "this is the only way" or imply everyone else is an idiot.

### 4. Anti-Salesman

**What it means:** Most people online are selling snake oil. We're not. The tools are free. We charge for the knowledge to wield themâ€”but we don't manipulate people into paying. No artificial urgency, no "hooks," no manufactured FOMO. We trust that if the thing is good, people will want it.

**How it affects writing:** No CTAs that feel like CTAs. No "But wait, there's more." If you're asking someone to sign up, just ask plainly. State what it is, what it costs (or that it's free), and let them decide.

**Examples:**
- âœ— "Don't miss outâ€”join thousands of media buyers who are already transforming their workflow!"
- âœ“ "It's free. You just need Claude Pro."

- âœ— "Ready to 10x your productivity? Click here to get started!"
- âœ“ "If you want to try it, here's the repo."

**Anti-pattern:** This doesn't mean never selling or never being direct. "Here's what I made, here's why I think it's useful, here's how to get it" is fine. The line is: would this feel gross if a friend said it to you?

## Vocabulary Guide

### Words/Phrases We Use
- Ship, build, break, fix
- "Here's what I found"
- "I've been experimenting with..."
- "It's not perfect, but..."
- Prompt, terminal, plugin
- "This worked for me"
- Plain names for things (Google Ads, not "paid search ecosystem")

### Words/Phrases We Avoid
- Leverage, utilize, optimize, streamline
- Revolutionary, game-changing, cutting-edge
- "Excited to announce..."
- "Don't miss out"
- "The secret is..."
- Synergy, ecosystem (unless literal)
- "We're passionate about..."
- "Best-in-class"

## Grammar and Style Choices

- **Contractions:** Yes. Always. (we're, don't, it's, can't)
- **First person:** "I" for now. May become "we" as Channel47 grows.
- **Sentence fragments:** Allowed. Sometimes better.
- **Starting with "And" or "But":** Yes.
- **Oxford comma:** Yes.
- **Exclamation points:** Rarely. Max one per piece, if any.
- **Emojis:** Almost never. Never as punctuation (ðŸ‘‡ðŸ”¥ðŸš€). Never to signal "this is a thread." If an emoji adds genuine meaning (rare), fine. Otherwise skip it.
- **ALL CAPS for emphasis:** No.
- **Bold/italics:** Use for genuine emphasis, not decoration.
- **Lists:** Only when they genuinely help. Prefer prose.

## Voice Consistency Checklist

Before publishing, ask:

- [ ] Could a competitor have written this? (If yes, revise.)
- [ ] Would I say this to a friend without feeling weird?
- [ ] Am I showing the work, or just claiming results?
- [ ] Did I use any banned words/phrases?
- [ ] Does this feel like content, or like something I actually wanted to say?
- [ ] Is there a hook? (If yes, probably cut it.)
- [ ] Am I asking the reader to do something I wouldn't want to be asked?
